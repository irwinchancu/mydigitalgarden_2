# Find a Mentor at Work

Adapted from “What’s the Right Way to Find a Mentor?” by Janet T. Phan

|   |
|---|
|READ MORE<br><br>_Work Smart: Bosses, Coworkers, and Building Great Work Relationships_<br><br>**Chapter 19  <br>**What’s the Right Way to Find a Mentor?|

A good mentor can make a huge imprint on your life. They can put you in the driver’s seat of your career, empower you to explore options that previously seemed unattainable, give you access to untapped opportunities, and teach you how to navigate challenges. Use this worksheet to find mentors and to maintain and nurture those relationships.

## Ask for a Meeting

A suitable mentor is trustworthy. They advocate for you and offer you advice. After you identify someone who might be a suitable mentor, first, reach out to them by asking for a quick 15- to 30-minute meeting. Use the following email script to do so, replacing the highlighted text with your own responses:

_Dear X,_

_I’ve been reading about the work you’re doing with Y. I’m interested in building my career in Z, and I’d love to hear about your experience. Would it be possible for us to have a quick video chat sometime within the next couple of weeks?_

A first meeting should be low commitment for your target mentor but will give you an opportunity to better understand them, gauge your chemistry, and see if they’d be the right fit.

## Nurture the Relationship

Your first meeting is an opportunity for casual conversation with your potential mentor. Brainstorm a few questions you can ask them to deepen the relationship. For example, “What do you like to do on the weekends?,” “What books do you like to read?,” or “What hobbies are you interested in?”

## Send a Thank-You Note

After meeting with your potential mentor, follow up with a thank-you email sometime within the same week. In your message, share several things you learned during the meeting or conversation. Use the following email script to do so, replacing the highlighted text with your own responses:

_Dear X,_

_I loved learning that Y. I was also surprised to hear that Z. If it’s okay with you, I’d like to follow up with you in a few weeks to continue our discussion._

Three to five weeks after sending the thank-you message, follow up to let your potential mentor know what you did in light of your discussions from that first meeting. Ask if they would be willing to meet up again within the next couple of weeks.

  
## Maintain the Relationship

Show your mentor that their time is being used well by demonstrating that you are providing a return on their investment. Send them occasional updates, offer to help them with projects, and express your gratitude for their guidance.
