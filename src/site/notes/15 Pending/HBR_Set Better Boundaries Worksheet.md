The most balanced and productive people know how to set boundaries—they know where the line is between their personal and professional lives, so they can get work done without sacrificing their personal time. To set better boundaries in your day-to-day life, follow the steps below.

## Step One: Establish Working Hours

If you want to have a focused day of work, determine your active working hours, whether you work in person or remotely (perhaps it’s 9 a.m. to 6 p.m. on most weekdays), and clarify what is or isn’t acceptable to do during this time. ## Step Two: Structure Your Day for Success

Maximize the effectiveness of your time by structuring each workday.

For example, make one day a week a meeting-free day. If you can’t avoid meetings entirely, reserve at least half a day for focused work. Choose a time that works best for you, keeping any required meetings and your energy levels in mind. Then, define one or two key items that you want to accomplish during this time. These could be tasks that require an hour or more of uninterrupted attention, or they could be items that require more creative, strategic thinking than you may be able to achieve in the office environment.

## Step Three: Set Boundaries with Others

Be clear with the people you interact with during the day (this set of people might vary if you’re fully remote, hybrid, or in-office). Explain to colleagues, friends, family, and other relevant parties that the hours dedicated to focused work aren’t opportunities for nonwork-related activities.

For example, if you’re working remotely, here is a sample script to share with your partner or roommate:

_“I’m planning on being on my computer from xx a.m. to xx p.m. today. I’m happy to chat at lunch, but otherwise I’ll be occupied.”_

In situations where you may have unexpected visitors (a colleague stops by your desk, someone knocks on your door), be open to a short conversation. Don’t suggest they come in for coffee or have an extended discussion. Here is a sample exit line you can use:

_“It was so wonderful to talk with you, but I’ve got some work to finish up.”_

If you need to take on nonwork requests like errands, estimate what you can do during a lunch hour. Here is a sample script you can use:

_“I’m happy to do xx at lunch, but I won’t have time for xx until after work.”_

Or:

_“I can do xx today, but I won’t get to xx until tomorrow.”_
